# Scene Note — The Unveiling (Chapter II, close)

The hinge of the book.

The three of them are back in the studio. Basil sets down his brushes.
The portrait is finished. He turns the easel so that Dorian can see it.

Beat structure:

1. **Recognition.** Dorian sees himself for the first time as he
   appears to others. He stands a long moment. He says nothing.
2. **The cold thought.** He understands, all at once, that the face
   on the canvas will not change and his own will. The thought has
   been planted in him through the whole scene by Lord Henry. It now
   germinates in his own voice.
3. **The wish.** He says it aloud. He would give his soul if it could
   be the other way round — if the picture could grow old and he
   could stay as he is now.
4. **Basil's reaction.** Basil is wounded. He had not understood
   until this moment that his picture had become a danger to the boy
   he loves. He moves toward the canvas — in v2, with a palette knife;
   the question of whether he actually intends to destroy it is to be
   left ambiguous on the page. Dorian stops him, with surprising
   strength.
5. **Lord Henry intervenes.** He is amused, and a little startled.
   He does what he does best: he turns the moment into a witticism
   and defuses it. The picture is preserved. The wish, however, is
   not unsaid.

What the reader is to feel at the end of the scene: that something
has happened in the room which none of the three understands. The
picture has been changed. It will not be revealed yet that it has
been changed. The reader is left holding the suspicion alone.

This scene is the test of the whole opening movement. If it does not
land, nothing else in the book will. It must be written cold, sober,
and short. No ornament. The room is doing the work.
