# Scene Note — The Studio (Chapter I, opening)

The scene is two men in a room, a finished portrait between them, and
a question — *will you exhibit it?* — that is the surface of a deeper
question Basil cannot bring himself to answer.

The room is in summer. The reader should be aware of the garden
through the open door before they are aware of the canvas. Smell
first, then sight. The order matters.

Lord Henry is not to move much. He is on the divan; he stays there
for almost the whole scene. He gestures with the cigarette. The
inertia is part of his menace.

Basil moves. Basil paces, returns to the canvas, looks at it, looks
away from it. His hands are restless. He should not be allowed to
settle.

The argument runs on three levels at once:

1. Should the picture be exhibited? (the surface)
2. What did the painter put into the picture? (the middle)
3. What does the painter feel for his subject? (the depth)

Only the first is named. The second is danced around. The third is
not named at all and must be heard between the sentences.

By the end of the scene Lord Henry has heard about Dorian for the
first time and has decided he wants to meet him. Basil has agreed,
unhappily, that he will be introduced. The arrival of Dorian himself
is the next scene's hinge.

Length on the page: long enough that Lord Henry has *room*. He
needs a paragraph or two to deliver his theory of influence. The
scene cannot be cut shorter than that without losing its function,
which is to make the reader fall a little in love with him before
Dorian does.
