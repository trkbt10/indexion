# Plot Outline — v1 (initial draft)

Working title: *The Picture of Dorian Gray*
Scope: Chapters I–III (the studio, the sitting, and the inquiry into the boy's lineage).

The aim of this draft is to set out, plainly, the shape of the opening
movement before any prose is committed to paper. It is to be read as a
skeleton only — atmosphere, dialogue, and ornament are reserved for the
writing itself.

---

## Act 1 — The Studio in Summer

- The painter Basil Hallward stands before a finished portrait of an
  uncommonly beautiful young man. The studio opens into a walled garden
  in a quiet corner of London; lilac and laburnum are in flower.
- Lord Henry Wotton, Hallward's friend, is sprawled on a divan smoking.
  He admires the portrait and presses Basil to send it to the Grosvenor.
- Basil refuses. The reason he gives — that he has "put too much of
  himself" into the canvas — is true, but he does not wish to be pressed
  on what he means by it.
- Beat: a **silver cigarette case** that Basil keeps on the side-table is
  picked up and turned over by Lord Henry as he speaks. It belongs to
  the painter's late father and is the small, concrete thing Basil
  touches whenever he feels exposed.

## Act 2 — The Painter's Confession

- Pressed, Basil tells Lord Henry how he first met Dorian at Lady
  Brandon's crush. He felt, on first sight, that this face would absorb
  his whole nature as an artist if he allowed it.
- His refusal to exhibit the picture is the refusal of a man unwilling
  to be read by strangers. He fears the painting tells the world what
  he himself cannot say aloud.
- Lord Henry, half-amused and half-curious, asks to be introduced to
  the boy. Basil resists; he does not want Henry's influence to touch
  Dorian. He says so plainly.

## Act 3 — Dorian Arrives

- Dorian Gray comes to the studio for a sitting. He is at the piano
  when the two men come in from the garden. Basil tries to send Lord
  Henry away. Dorian, polite but already curious, asks him to stay.
- During the sitting Lord Henry talks. He talks about youth, about the
  poverty of an unlived life, about the body as the soul's vehicle.
  Dorian does not quite know what is happening to him, only that
  something is.
- The painter works in near-silence and is dimly aware that the room
  has changed.

## Act 4 — The Garden and the Wish

- Basil calls a halt. Lord Henry takes Dorian into the garden. Among
  the lilacs he gives a long speech in praise of beauty and against the
  waste of it. The boy is afraid, and stirred, and does not know which
  feeling is which.
- They return to the studio. The portrait is finished. Dorian, seeing
  it, understands for the first time that he is beautiful, and that
  beauty will not last.
- He says aloud, with a sudden child's bitterness, that he would give
  his soul if the picture could grow old in his place. The wish is the
  hinge of the book. Basil and Lord Henry hear it without understanding
  what has been said in the room.

## Act 5 — The Uncle in the Albany

- The next day, Lord Henry calls on his uncle, Lord Fermor, in the
  Albany rooms. The old bachelor is grumpy, fond of his nephew, and
  furnished with the entire scandal of London for the last forty years.
- Henry asks about Dorian's mother. Fermor tells the story: Lady
  Margaret Devereux, a beauty, ran away with a penniless subaltern; the
  husband was killed in a duel arranged by her father, Lord Kelso; the
  girl died within a year; the boy is the heir.
- Henry leaves with the lineage in hand. He thinks of Dorian as a kind
  of experiment now, and goes to lunch at his Aunt Agatha's.

## Beats reserved for later acts (not in scope)

- The studio's **velvet curtain** drawn over the finished portrait.
- The actress, **Sibyl Vane**, of the cheap theatre off the Strand.
- The yellow book Lord Henry will lend Dorian.
