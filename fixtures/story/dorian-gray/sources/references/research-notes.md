# Research Notes

A miscellany. These are the things looked up while planning the book.
They are not all to be used. Some are here because they sharpen the
writer's own sense of the period; others mark places where, if a
detail is needed, the right detail is to be reached for, not invented.

---

## Aestheticism, the Grosvenor, and the magazines

- The Aesthetic movement of the 1870s and 1880s — Pater, Whistler,
  Burne-Jones, Wilde himself in the lectures of '82 — gives Lord
  Henry his idiom. The slogan to keep in mind is Pater's "to burn
  always with this hard, gemlike flame." Lord Henry is, in the studio
  scene, a debased and witty popularizer of Pater.
- The Grosvenor Gallery was founded by Sir Coutts Lindsay in 1877 as
  an alternative to the Royal Academy. It is the gallery for the
  Aesthetic school. By 1890 it is past its zenith but still the right
  place for an ambitious portrait of an unfashionable subject.
- The *Yellow Book* is post-1894 and therefore anachronistic for an
  1890 setting. It is **not** to be referred to in the prose of these
  chapters. Where Lord Henry hands Dorian a book, it is to be a
  yellow-covered French novel from Paris, not the *Yellow Book*. (The
  distinction matters.)

## The Faustian frame

- The Faust legend: a man trades his soul for a temporal good; the
  bill comes due. Marlowe and Goethe are the obvious models. The
  point of departure here is that the bargain is *not* struck with a
  named devil; it is struck almost as an accident, by a young man too
  inexperienced to know he is striking a bargain at all.
- Lord Henry is **not** Mephistopheles. He is the occasion of the
  bargain, not its other party. He does not himself know what has
  happened in the studio when Dorian speaks the wish. This is to be
  preserved in the prose: Henry hears the speech as charming and
  juvenile.
- The portrait itself is the contracting party. This is the book's
  invention.

## Victorian portrait practice

- A formal full-length, life-size portrait was, in 1890, the work of
  weeks or months, sat in repeated sittings of an hour or two. The
  subject stood on a low platform. A large canvas was the rule for a
  formal subject; for Dorian, working assumption is roughly seven
  feet by four.
- Pigments: lead white, vermilion, the cadmiums (then new), the
  earths. A painter of Hallward's seriousness would grind his own or
  buy from Roberson's of Long Acre. This is detail for the writer's
  own confidence, not for the page.

## Mayfair and the Albany

- Mayfair: the parallelogram bounded by Oxford Street, Park Lane,
  Piccadilly, and Regent Street. Curzon Street runs east–west through
  the middle. A walk from Curzon Street to the Albany is short — about
  ten minutes at the pace a man of leisure walks at half-past noon.
- The Albany: a set of bachelor chambers occupying what was Lord
  Melbourne's house behind Burlington Gardens. By 1890 it is the
  London address of confirmed bachelors of a certain class. To "have
  rooms in the Albany" is shorthand for a particular sort of life.

## Hellenism and the cult of beauty

- Antinous, the favourite of Hadrian, drowned in the Nile in 130 AD,
  was made by his Emperor into a god and is the most reproduced face
  of late antiquity. To compare a young man in 1890 to Antinous is to
  say something specific about him, and about the speaker.
- Narcissus, similarly: the boy who fell in love with his own
  reflection. Lord Henry's joke in Chapter I — that Dorian is a
  Narcissus and Basil is a Faun, or some such — is to have its bite.
- Adonis: dead at the height of his beauty, gored by a boar. Used as
  a passing ornament in the studio scene; not load-bearing.

## Victorian morality and the "decadent" panic

- The 1885 Criminal Law Amendment Act (Labouchère Amendment) makes
  certain things between men criminal regardless of consent. By 1890
  this is the legal background against which any "passionate
  friendship" between men of the educated class must be read. Basil's
  reluctance to exhibit the picture — his fear that he has "shown the
  secret of his own soul" — works at this level whether or not the
  reader chooses to see it.
- The press is hostile to "decadence." Henley's *National Observer*
  and the *St. James's Gazette* are the journals to expect attacks
  from when the book appears. (This is for the writer's benefit; the
  fictional reception is not the present concern.)

## A note on the cigarette case

- The detail of a silver cigarette case in v1 of the outline was
  borrowed, half-consciously, from a recent case in the courts. It
  has been struck out of v2 to avoid a too-direct reference. The
  painter's nervousness is to be carried by other means.

## A small list of things *not* to do

- Do not have Lord Henry quote French. He may *think* in French; he
  does not quote.
- Do not name the street the studio is on.
- Do not show Sibyl Vane until the chapter that is hers.
- Do not let Basil say outright that he loves Dorian. He says it
  between his sentences. That is the whole point.
