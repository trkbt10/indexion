# Character Sheet

These are working notes. They are not meant to appear on the page in
this form. Their purpose is to keep the figures consistent across
chapters and to record decisions about each one before drafting.

---

## Dorian Gray

- **Age at first appearance:** twenty.
- **Appearance:** fair, almost golden hair; blue eyes; a complexion
  that the painter compares, half-mockingly, to ivory and rose-leaves.
  Tall, but not heavy. He carries himself like someone who has not
  yet had occasion to be aware of his body.
- **Background:** orphaned. Raised by a hard, miserly grandfather
  (Lord Kelso). His mother, Lady Margaret Devereux, died within a year
  of his father. He has come into a small fortune through her side of
  the family. He has the country house at Selby and is not yet of age
  in the legal sense — he is to come into the bulk of the estate at
  twenty-one.
- **Temperament:** ingenuous, easily moved, vain in the way that very
  young people are vain when they have not yet been told they are
  beautiful. He plays the piano well — Schumann's *Forest Scenes* is
  in his hands when Lord Henry first sees him.
- **Speech habits:** he addresses older men, including Basil, with an
  unforced "you" rather than the more deferential forms a stricter
  upbringing would have given him.
- **Function in this opening movement:** he is the canvas onto which
  Lord Henry paints. By the end of Chapter II he has spoken the wish
  that will run the rest of the book.

## Basil Hallward

- **Age:** in his thirties.
- **Profession:** painter, of the serious sort. He has shown at the
  Grosvenor Gallery before, and once at the Academy, which he disliked.
- **Appearance:** a strong, plain face; rugged, in the painter's own
  estimation. Dark hair beginning to be touched. Hands that show his
  trade.
- **Temperament:** affectionate, anxious, given to plain speaking
  except on the one subject — Dorian — about which he cannot quite
  bring himself to be plain.
- **Studio:** large room with a north light, opening through a wide
  door into the walled garden. A divan covered in Persian saddle-bags;
  an easel; a velvet curtain to draw across finished work.
- **Moral function:** the conscience of the book. He sees the danger
  Lord Henry represents and says so. He is overruled.

## Lord Henry Wotton

- **Age:** somewhere in his thirties; older than Basil by a little.
- **Position:** an idle aristocrat with literary tastes. Married,
  unhappily and decoratively, to a Lady Victoria. (His wife does not
  appear in Chapters I–III but should be available for later use.)
- **Appearance:** tall, languid, fastidiously dressed. He smokes
  constantly. He prefers cigarettes tainted with opium because they
  are *the* affectation of a certain set in 1890.
- **Speech:** paradox. Almost every speech is a paradox or a near-paradox.
  He speaks to provoke and to amuse himself. He does not always believe
  what he says, but he means it at the moment of speaking.
- **Function in this opening movement:** the corrupter. He is to deliver,
  in the studio and in the garden, the speech that plants in Dorian the
  fear of growing old. He must be charming throughout. The reader must
  wish to listen to him.

## Lord Fermor ("Uncle George")

- **Position:** Lord Henry's maternal uncle. A bachelor in his sixties.
  He keeps rooms in the Albany and rarely leaves them before two.
- **Appearance:** stout, ruddy, dressed loudly. The kind of old gentleman
  the clubs of St. James's still produce in some quantity.
- **Manner:** rough, kind under it, indiscreet about everyone else and
  guarded about himself. He is the convenient mouth through which the
  history of Dorian's parents is to reach the reader.
- **Function:** exposition, but exposition wrapped in personality so
  it does not feel like exposition. He is a man who speaks ill of his
  acquaintances out of affection.

## Lady Margaret Devereux (deceased)

- Dorian's mother. Spoken of in Chapter III only.
- A celebrated beauty of the previous generation. Ran away with a
  penniless subaltern in a foot regiment. Her father, Lord Kelso, is
  said to have arranged a duel in which the husband was killed.
- She returned to her father's house and died within a year of the
  duel. The boy was raised by Kelso under conditions Lord Fermor calls
  "cruel."
- She is the structural rhyme for Dorian: a beauty undone by a
  passion. Whether the prose explicitly draws the parallel is open.

## Sibyl Vane (not yet on stage)

- An actress at a low theatre on the south side of the river. To
  appear from Chapter IV onward.
- Mentioned here only so the character sheet remains complete. She is
  not to be referred to in the prose of Chapters I–III.

## Lord Kelso (deceased, mentioned)

- Dorian's grandfather. A miser. Hated his daughter's husband. Arranged
  the duel that killed him. Raised the orphaned grandson harshly.
- A figure of background dread. He does not appear directly.

## Mrs. Vandeleur

- A name to be kept available for the lunch at Aunt Agatha's. A duchess's
  cousin, of the kind who speaks earnestly about the East End.
