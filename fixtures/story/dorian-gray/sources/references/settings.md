# Setting Notes

Period: London, the summer of about 1890. The book is to feel
contemporary to its writing. Where actual streets and institutions
are named, they are to be named correctly; where they are not load-
bearing, they may be left vague.

---

## Basil Hallward's studio

- Location: a quiet street in the artists' quarter at the western
  edge of the parks. Working assumption: somewhere within walking
  distance of Holland Park, on the borders of Kensington. The exact
  street is not to be named in the prose.
- The studio itself is a long room with a glass roof on the north
  side, opening at one end through a wide door directly into a small
  walled garden. The garden is the one with the lilacs and the
  laburnum.
- Furniture of note:
  - the divan piled with Persian saddle-bags
  - the easel, set so the light falls across the canvas from the
    left
  - a velvet curtain on a brass rod that can be drawn across a
    finished picture
  - a low piano against the wall opposite the easel; this is where
    Dorian is found in Chapter II
- Atmosphere: in summer the studio smells of oil paint, of the lilac
  through the open door, and of Lord Henry's cigarettes.

## The garden behind the studio

- Walled in old brick. Roses on a south wall. A great laburnum,
  hanging its yellow, in the centre. Lilacs at the side. By the door,
  the **pink-flowering thorn** (May or June). The thorn is the
  sentinel; it is the first thing one smells when crossing the
  threshold.
- A path runs the length of the garden. Two green-and-white butterflies
  are noted on the day of the sitting; this is to be kept as a small,
  exact detail.

## Lord Henry's house, Mayfair

- **Curzon Street.** Lord Henry walks from Curzon Street to the Albany
  in Chapter III. The house is small for the address, in the way that
  fashionable London houses on famous streets often are.
- Interior: a library panelled in olive-stained oak with a cream-
  coloured frieze. Lord Henry does most of his persuading in this
  room when he is not doing it in someone else's drawing room. (Used
  in later chapters; not in the scope of I–III.)

## The Albany

- Piccadilly. The set of bachelor chambers off the courtyard behind
  the house. Lord Fermor keeps rooms here. They are stuffy, full of
  blue books he does not read, and smell of cigars and of the
  particular furniture polish used by his man.
- Note for the writer: The Albany is to be referred to, in dialogue
  and narration, simply as "the Albany" — not "Albany Court," not
  "the Albany Apartments." The local usage is bare.

## Berkeley Square

- Lord Henry's destination after the Albany. He is going on to lunch
  with his Aunt Agatha. The square is to be reached by a walk from
  Piccadilly through the back streets of Mayfair. The walk itself is
  glanced at, not described.

## Selby Royal

- The Gray country house, in (working assumption) Nottinghamshire.
  Mentioned only in Chapter III as part of Dorian's expectations
  under his mother's settlement. Not seen in the opening movement.

## The Grosvenor Gallery

- Bond Street. New Bond Street, more precisely. The gallery to which
  Lord Henry urges Basil to send the portrait. Founded 1877, by 1890
  past its first heat but still the place of the moment for the
  Aesthetic painters. Whistler exhibited there. The Academy is
  mentioned by Basil only to be dismissed.

## The cheap theatre (off-stage)

- A small theatre on the south side of the Thames, in the streets
  behind the Lambeth Road. To appear in Chapter IV. Mentioned in
  Chapter II only as "the theatre" Lord Henry proposes they go to;
  the name and the actress are deferred.

## Time of year

- Late May or early June. The thorn is in flower; the lilac is just
  going over. The days are long enough that the studio scene takes
  place in the late afternoon and is still in full light when it ends.
